There is a particular quality to mornings in late autumn that resists easy description. The light, even at midday, has a low and tentative character, as though the sun were rationing itself in anticipation of the months ahead. Frost lingers in shaded hollows long after the open ground has thawed. Birds that summered in the meadows have long since dispersed, and the ones that remain — jays, crows, the occasional sparrow hawk — move with a kind of conservation of effort that contrasts sharply with the easy abundance of June. There is no urgency in their movements, only economy. To walk through such a landscape is to feel that one has stepped outside the rhythm of growth and decay and into a kind of suspended interval, neither active nor dormant, in which the next event is always somewhere just over the next ridge. The path I had been following narrowed as it climbed, and the leaves underfoot, where they had not been trampled flat by previous walkers, crunched with a thin, glassy sound that suggested they had frozen and thawed several times already. I had not come prepared for the cold, having underestimated the elevation gain on the map, but the exertion of the climb kept me warm enough, and the gloves I had thrust into my coat pocket as an afterthought turned out to be sufficient. Above the treeline the wind picked up, scouring the ridge bare of any loose vegetation and pressing the dry grasses flat against the rock. From the summit one could see, on a clear day, three counties; today the visibility was less generous, but the nearer valleys were still distinct, each with its own pattern of fields and woodlots and small clustered settlements. I stopped to drink water and to rest my legs, which had begun to register the climb in earnest, and I thought about the route back down. There were two options: the way I had come, which was longer but better-marked, or a steeper descent down the western face, which would shave perhaps an hour off the return but which the guidebook described in terms that suggested a certain caution. I have always been suspicious of guidebooks that hedge — the hedge usually understates the difficulty by a meaningful margin — but I was also not in the mood to add an hour to a day that had already become longer than I had planned. Compromise being the foundation of all such decisions, I chose the western descent and resolved to take it slowly. The first half-mile was easier than advertised: a packed dirt track switching back through a stand of birches whose bare branches admitted enough light to keep the path clear. After the birches the trail dropped more steeply into a band of mixed conifers, and the footing became less reliable. Roots crossed the path at awkward angles, and the dirt, frozen in the shade of the trees, did not yield as predictably as soil normally does. I picked my way down, using a stick I had found near the summit as an improvised pole, and I was perhaps three-quarters of the way down the steepest pitch when I came around a turn and saw, in the crotch of a dead pine perhaps twenty feet off the trail, what I took at first to be a tangle of windblown debris but recognized, on second look, as the kestrel's nest I had heard the local naturalists describe two seasons earlier. It was unoccupied — kestrels do not winter at this latitude — but the structure itself was intact, and the angle of the late light caught its outline in a way that made the shape unmistakable. I stood watching it for several minutes, partly out of genuine interest and partly out of relief at having something to look at that did not require careful foot placement. The remainder of the descent was uneventful. The trail leveled into a meadow about a mile above the trailhead, and from there the walking was easy: a wide, gentle path through tall grass already gone yellow with the season. Sheep had been grazing in the upper pastures earlier in the year, and their tracks were still visible in places where the ground had been soft. I passed a low stone wall that someone had clearly rebuilt in the last year or two — the moss had not yet had time to colonize the new stones — and beyond the wall a small stand of apple trees, gone wild, with shrunken fruit still clinging to the higher branches. I considered taking one but the bruising looked extensive and I had no way to wash it. The trailhead was where I had left it, of course, though the car park had emptied considerably since the morning. A single late-model station wagon remained, and an older man was loading what looked like surveying equipment into the back of it. He nodded to me as I came down the last stretch of path, and I nodded back, and neither of us spoke. There is a particular etiquette at trailheads that I have come to appreciate: an acknowledgment that you have both been doing something that doesn't quite belong to ordinary daily life, combined with a tacit agreement not to make a fuss about it. I got into my own car, started the engine, and let it idle for a few minutes while I drank the rest of my water and ate the second of the two sandwiches I had packed. The drive home was the part of the day I had been least looking forward to — the road from the trailhead is narrow and winding and unpleasant to drive in fading light — but it was no worse than I had feared, and by the time I reached the main road the sun had set enough that the oncoming headlights had begun to outline the curves ahead, which is actually easier to drive by than the ambiguous in-between light of true dusk. I thought, on the way home, about how rarely I now took days like this one, and how much I had gained from this one in particular, and I resolved — as I always do after such days, and as I always fail to follow through on — to take another one within the month. The resolution lasted as long as such resolutions usually do, which is to say not long, but the day itself remained intact in memory, and weeks later I could still recall, with surprising specificity, the angle of the light on the dead pine and the shape of the nest in its crotch. Memory selects strangely. There are days I have spent in elaborate and supposedly memorable circumstances that have left almost no trace, and ordinary afternoons whose details I can reconstruct decades later. Before going further into that observation, though, I want to record a few more details from the day itself, because the details are the substance and the reflection is only the framing. On the lower meadow, between the apple trees and the trailhead, I noticed a series of small piles of stones arranged at irregular intervals along one side of the path. They were not cairns in the navigational sense — the path was wide and obvious and required no marking — and they were too neatly constructed to be the accidental product of someone clearing a field. I counted seven of them over a stretch of perhaps two hundred yards, and then, having grown curious, I stopped counting and started looking more carefully at the construction. Each pile was about knee-high, built of the local sandstone, and arranged with a flat capstone on top. The stones had a weathered look that suggested the piles were at least several years old, possibly older. I have since asked two people who know the area better than I do, and neither could account for them with any confidence. One suggested they were a remnant of a livestock-counting practice that had fallen out of use; the other thought they might be commemorative, marking the spots where someone had died on the trail in earlier centuries. Both explanations seemed plausible and neither was satisfying. I have come to suspect that there is no single answer, and that the piles persist because they are sufficiently inert to outlast the practices that produced them — like the stone walls in the woods, which everyone agrees are old but no one can quite date. There is a literature on this kind of landscape feature, I gather, which I have not yet bothered to read but which I have noted as worth pursuing if I ever find myself with a long winter and not enough to do. Beyond the piles, the path crossed a small culvert where a seasonal stream presumably ran in wetter months. The streambed was dry now, paved with smooth river stones and one or two larger boulders that the stream had apparently failed to move during whatever its most ambitious flooding had been. I stopped at the culvert for a moment because the surface under it was the first level ground I had encountered since the meadow began, and because I wanted to retie my left bootlace, which had loosened on the descent and which I had been ignoring out of laziness rather than because the discomfort was below the threshold of action. While I was crouched there I noticed that someone had carved initials into the wooden plank that served as the upper rail of the culvert. The initials were weathered nearly smooth and I could not read them, but the carving was deep enough to feel under a thumb, and I spent perhaps a full minute running my hand along the indentation and trying to decide whether the letters had been A and M or perhaps H and M or H and W. The exercise was unresolvable but pleasant, in the way that such small puzzles can be pleasant when there is no consequence to getting them wrong. From the culvert the last stretch to the car park was uneventful. I noticed a few late wildflowers in a sheltered patch beside the trail — purple ones that I could not identify and that I have since failed to identify from photographs, which is itself a small ongoing irritation — and I noticed that the wind, which had been steady on the ridge, had dropped almost entirely in the protected lower section. The temperature was correspondingly higher, and I unzipped my jacket and pushed back the sleeves of my undershirt before continuing the last quarter-mile, which I have noted above. I want to mention one further detail before returning to the larger observation about memory. As I was loading my pack into the trunk of the car, I noticed that the surveyor's station wagon had a license plate from a state two over from the one I live in, and I wondered briefly what had brought him so far afield. The equipment in his car suggested professional rather than hobbyist work, and I considered, for the length of a heartbeat, asking him what he was surveying. I did not, because asking would have violated the trailhead etiquette I described earlier, and because the question was idle on my end and might have been imposing on his. But I have continued to think about it occasionally, and I find that the unasked question has acquired a kind of permanence in my memory that an answered question would not have. There is something to that, though I am not sure what. Perhaps it is only that an open question stays alive longer than a closed one, the way an unfinished song lodges in the head more persistently than a song you have heard all the way through. Or perhaps it is that the imagined answers — surveying for a new trail, surveying for a property dispute, surveying for some old boundary that needed re-establishing for legal purposes — are each more interesting than whatever the actual answer would have been, and the absence of a single correct answer leaves the field open for all of them. I think this is the same mechanism by which the kinds of mysteries that get solved are less memorable than the kinds that don't, which is one of the reasons that detective fiction, taken in long enough doses, comes to feel weirdly less satisfying than ordinary fiction, despite or because of its higher event density. Anyway. I think this asymmetry is not really about the days themselves but about the attentional state one was in at the time. The days I remember most clearly are the ones during which I was, for whatever reason, paying close attention to the texture of being there: not preoccupied with what came before or what would come next, not running a parallel internal monologue, not reaching for the next stimulus before the current one had finished arriving. These are not necessarily the days of high stakes. They are, more often, the days of low stakes and high attention, which is an unusual combination and which I have come to suspect is the actual prerequisite for the kind of experience that lodges in memory and becomes available for retrieval. The descent past the dead pine qualified. The car park exchange with the surveyor qualified. The bruised apples did not, quite, qualify — I noticed them but I was already calculating distance to the car — and they have already begun to fade. In any case, I am writing this several weeks after the fact, and the writing itself is, I think, a kind of attentional act, a refusal to let the day's contents slip back into the undifferentiated pile of recent past. Whether this kind of writing is worth doing for its own sake or only as an instrumental practice — a hedge against forgetting, or a rehearsal for later writing — I genuinely don't know, and the question may not have a useful answer. What I do know is that the days I have written about are the ones I can still describe years later, and the days I have not written about have, almost without exception, become inaccessible to me except as the vaguest possible outline. The mechanism is probably more about retrieval than about storage: the writing creates the cues by which the memory becomes findable again. Without the cues, the memory is presumably still in there somewhere, but it might as well not be. Which suggests that the value of this kind of writing is not in the act of producing the document — the document itself I will probably never reread — but in the act of laying down the cues. I find this an oddly consoling thought. It means that the apparent waste of time involved in writing things down at length, when I could have been doing almost anything else, is not actually waste at all, but a long-deferred investment in the future accessibility of the present. The present, after all, becomes the past with no input from us, and the past is mostly inert unless we have done the work of making it findable. The work of making it findable is what writing is for, or one of the things it is for, and the trail that descends past that solitary tree is now findable in a way it would not have been if I had let the day pass without this kind of attention. That is enough, I think, to justify the time spent writing this, even — perhaps especially — if no one ever reads it but me.
